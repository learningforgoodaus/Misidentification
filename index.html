Why Victims of DFV are Misidentified

Victims who belong to groups facing extra challenges are often wrongly identified as the person causing harm. This happens due to unfair views, a lack of understanding, or their unique situation. Click on a group below to see the specific problems and warning signs that services often miss.

Aboriginal Women

Why They Slip Through the Net (Misidentification)

Unfair Views & Historical Hurt: Police and services might unfairly see Aboriginal people as offenders because of past over-policing. They may not understand the long-lasting effects of historical events or how DFV looks in a complex family setting.

Signs Often Missed

Forced to Cooperate: The victim might not report the harm, or they might even protect the perpetrator because they fear their children will be taken away by child protection services (a common historical fear). Services mistake this forced cooperation for shared fault or shared blame.

Refugees and Migrants

Why They Slip Through the Net (Misidentification)

Visa Threats & Language Problems: The person causing harm often threatens to have the victim deported, cancel their visa sponsorship, or takes advantage of the victim not understanding Australian law. If a victim acts urgently to get their documents back, this can be wrongly seen as aggression or unstable behaviour.

Signs Often Missed

Alone and Controlled: The harm often involves total control over money, documents, and who the victim can talk to. The victim may seem totally dependent or very stressed. Services may wrongly call this a personality issue instead of a reaction to abuse.

People with Disabilities

Why They Slip Through the Net (Misidentification)

The victim's condition, how they communicate, or the need to rely on the perpetrator (who is often their carer) is seen as the main problem. If the victim fights back to defend themselves, the services might blame this on the disability itself, leading to the victim being wrongly labelled as the aggressor.

Signs Often Missed

Forced Silence & Isolation: The victim has very few friends or contacts outside the home. Authorities may ignore their strong sadness or fear, saying it is simply part of their disability (e.g., "just worry" or "standard behaviour"). The abuse is often not checked for on its own.

LGBTQIA+ People

Why They Slip Through the Net (Misidentification)

In same-sex relationships, police and courts often wrongly view the violence as 'shared responsibility' because they assume that abuse only happens in straight relationships. The perpetrator threatens to reveal the victim's sexuality or gender identity to family or work, which makes the victim fight back against the threats. This act of self-protection is then recorded as an offense.

Signs Often Missed

Separated from Support: The abuse includes attacking the victim’s gender identity or sexuality (e.g., "No one will believe a gay person is abused"). The victim pulls away from their community and tries hard to hide information, which services wrongly see as being difficult or avoiding the truth.

Young People (16-25)

Why They Slip Through the Net (Misidentification)

The victim is often seen as just being a 'rebellious teenager' or a 'difficult partner' in a relationship. Because they don't have their own money or a safe place to live, they act desperately to stay in the abusive situation. Services mistake this desperation for immaturity or sharing the blame for the conflict.

Signs Often Missed

Inexperience & Online Abuse: Abuse often happens mostly online (sharing private photos, tracking location). Services looking for physical violence may not see this as DFV. The victim’s urgent attempts to delete things or escape the situation are misidentified as being restless or unstable.
