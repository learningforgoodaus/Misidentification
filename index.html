Why Some Victims Slip Through the Net

Victims who belong to groups facing extra challenges are often wrongly identified as the person causing harm. This happens due to unfair views, a lack of understanding, or their unique situation. Click on a group below to see the specific problems and warning signs that services often miss.

Aboriginal Women

Why They Slip Through the Net

Here are some of the reasons and circumstances that pose as barriers for Aboriginal women and children: A lack of support to understand legal rights and how to access support – especially in remote communities, lack of cultural competency in the support sector, lack of access to communication or transport, fear of child removal if disclosing DFV, community driven pressure to discourage people from seeking outside support.

Signs Often Missed

Frequent unexplained injuries from physical violence and hospital visits or crisis centers. Sudden or repeated displacement or homelessness, psychological distress – particularly among mothers who experienced conflict or family violence during pregnancy.

Isolation and reluctance to speak about what’s happening, or statements like “It’s just family business,” “We’ll handle it ourselves,” or “Don’t get outsiders involved.”

Source: Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people - Australian Institute of Health and Welfare

Refugees & Migrants

Why They Slip Through the Net

People who come to Australian from culturally and linguistically diverse backgrounds may face lack of access to information such as legal rights, language services and service availability. Because of this there can be fear and distrust of authorities (because of experiences in their country of origin), there may be community norms that discourage telling anyone outside the cultural group about violence in relationships.

Signs Often Missed

Seeming anxious and fearful because someone may be threatening that their visa will be withdrawn if they don’t comply with abuse. Hearing discussions about plans for female genital mutilation or cutting. Reproductive coercion and forced or underage marriage. Religious abuse – justifying a reduction in their human rights with certain religious beliefs.

Source: People from culturally and linguistically diverse backgrounds - Australian Institute of Health and Welfare

People with Disabilities

Why They Slip Through the Net

People with a disability experience the same barriers to seeking support for DFV, though their distinct barriers come from their increased physical and mental vulnerabilities. Because of this, they may not trust they will be taken seriously or believed, the feelings of shame, lack of access to information, inadequate specialised support services. Fear of negative consequences or losing care services.

Signs Often Missed

The person appears unusually withdrawn with a drop in confidence and willingness to engage in community activities. You might overhear their partner or carer make belittling comments, mocking their disability.

They may have unpaid bills, and lack of access to finances to purchase essential items like mobility aids, food and medication. Being left alone without food, medication, or support for extended periods. Some perpetrators may control when they can shower, who can visit or when they get to leave the house.

Source: People with disability - Australian Institute of Health and Welfare

LGBTQI+ People

Why They Slip Through the Net

LGBTQI+ people may find it difficult to access services for families if they feel they don’t fit into that category of support services offered. They may fear discrimination or of someone “outing” their gender status, sexuality or HIV status.

Signs Often Missed

The person may appear tense or worried when their phone messages are looked at my others. They may mention someone is threatening to use their sexuality as a threat against them. Sudden homelessness or signs of loneliness or isolation that appeared suddenly after disclosing their identity.

The person may speak out about sexual violence that occurred after someone commented on their identity or be forced into treatment practices that they do not want to do.

Source: LGBTIQA+ people - Australian Institute of Health and Welfare

Young People (16-25)

Why They Slip Through the Net

Some children and young people may be hesitant to speak up and report domestic and family violence as they are still young and may be perceived to be “making up stories”. While under legal parental care, they may fear the withdrawal of support for their basic need to be met (for example when abuse is perpetrated by a parent). Some may not be confident to express or communicate the abuse or not know who to trust or where to find services that can help them.

Signs Often Missed

Risk taking behaviours and substance abuse from trying to deal with trauma. Running away or homelessness in an attempt to remove themselves from an unsafe environment. Unexplained injuries, cigarette burns or frequent complaints of pain from physical violence.

Signs of neglect may be poor hygiene over an extended period. Social signs of control or intimidation may be a lack of trust for adults or authority. A decline in academic effort, disrupted sleep and depression.

Source: Children and young people - Australian Institute of Health and Welfare

Men

Why They Slip Through the Net

Men may face additional barriers to being recognised as victims because of stereotypes about masculinity or assumptions that they are more likely to be the person causing harm. They are often reluctant to disclose abuse, these reasons make it much harder for them to get the right help, protection, and support from police, courts, and other services.

Signs Often Missed

Because people may not expect men to be victims, important indicators can be missed such as withdrawal from friends and family, sudden changes in behaviour, depression and suicidal thoughts. Visible or unexplained injuries, or heightened anxiety when communicating with a partner.

Coercive control, physical and emotional abuse, financial restriction, or threats to reputation can also be overlooked because they don’t fit common stereotypes of what victimisation “looks like.”

Source: Who uses violence? - Australian Institute of Health and Welfare
